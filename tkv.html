Tokyo Vice

It's a reasonably tasty hand roll of yakuza drama and turn-of-the-millennium American coming-of-age tale.

HBO Max's Tokyo Vice is a Thrilling Newspaper Noir.

"Tokyo Vice" is a crime drama series based on the memoirs of American journalist Jake Adelstein. The show follows Adelstein's real-life experiences as a reporter covering the criminal underworld in Tokyo, Japan, and his personal struggles with the culture shock and ethical dilemmas that arise during his investigations.

Overall, "Tokyo Vice" is a well-crafted and engaging crime drama that explores the complex and often brutal world of organized crime in Japan. The show benefits from strong performances by its cast, particularly Ansel Elgort as Jake Adelstein and Ken Watanabe as his mentor, seasoned detective Hiroshi Shimizu.

The show's attention to detail and authenticity is also a standout feature, as it immerses viewers in the sights and sounds of Tokyo's criminal underworld. The series does not shy away from the darker aspects of its subject matter, and it presents a nuanced and thought-provoking exploration of the cultural clashes and moral dilemmas that arise when reporting on organized crime.

One of the strengths of the series is its pacing, as it balances the character development and personal stories of its protagonists with the more procedural aspects of crime reporting. The show also benefits from its high production values, with impressive cinematography and a great soundtrack.

However, the show's commitment to realism and authenticity can sometimes make it feel slow-paced, and some viewers may find the series a bit too heavy-handed in its messaging. Additionally, some of the supporting characters are not as well-developed as the main protagonists, and their stories can feel a bit underwhelming.

Overall, "Tokyo Vice" is a compelling and well-made crime drama that offers a unique and insightful look into the criminal underworld of Japan. With strong performances, impressive production values, and a thought-provoking exploration of its subject matter, the series is definitely worth watching for fans of the genre.
